1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account

Websites should be accessible as possible so that no one is left out. This could mean adapting your website to account for these disabilities: Auditory, cognitive, neurological, physical, speech and visual. This could mean adding features that will read the text on a page or read the button that the mouse is over so that blind or semi blind people can use and gather information from the website. People with colour blindness may have their own colour sheet or have changed the settings on the computer to make the user experience better for them. This can be helped by making sure text which contrasts with the background. Deaf people can struggle if there is visual content and there is a lack of captions. There are many small details that can be changed to make life easier for these people. People with physical disabilities may have difficulty using a keyboard, or find it difficult to use a mouse, and will need to rely on the keyboard for Web browsing. Some people will use adaptive technology with their computer to access the Web. Less severe disabilities could make the task of highlighting, manipulating pull-down boxes or mouse clicks difficult.

Filetypes for websites include:
